Anaphylaxis Protocol

Indications

Evidence of severe allergic reaction with compromise of airway, breathing, or circulation following exposure to allergen.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Respiratory: Dyspnea, bronchospasm, stridor, airway edema
  • Cardiovascular: Hypotension, tachycardia, shock
  • Skin: Urticaria, angioedema, flushing, pruritus
  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping
  • Neurological: Anxiety, sense of impending doom, altered level of consciousness

Treatment Protocol

Immediate Actions

  • Remove allergen if possible and safe to do so
  • Position patient supine with legs elevated (if tolerated)
  • Call for assistance
  • Administer high-flow oxygen (15 L/min via non-rebreather mask)
  • Monitor vital signs continuously
  • Obtain IV or IO access

Primary Medication

Epinephrine 1:1000 (1 mg/mL)

  • Adult dose: 0.3-0.5 mg IM (anterolateral thigh)
  • Pediatric dose: 0.01 mg/kg IM (maximum 0.3 mg)
  • May repeat every 5-15 minutes as needed
  • If no response after 2 doses, consider epinephrine infusion

Secondary Medications

Fluid Resuscitation

  • Normal saline 0.9% IV bolus
  • Adult: 1-2 liters rapid infusion
  • Pediatric: 20 mL/kg bolus

Antihistamines

  • Diphenhydramine 25-50 mg IV/IM (adult)
  • Pediatric: 1 mg/kg (maximum 50 mg)

Corticosteroids

  • Hydrocortisone 100-200 mg IV (adult)
  • Or Methylprednisolone 125 mg IV (adult)
  • Pediatric: 1-2 mg/kg (maximum 125 mg)

Bronchodilators (if bronchospasm present)

  • Salbutamol 2.5-5 mg via nebulizer
  • May repeat as needed

Refractory Anaphylaxis

If no response to initial treatment:

  • Continue epinephrine every 5 minutes
  • Consider epinephrine infusion (2-10 mcg/min)
  • Aggressive fluid resuscitation
  • Prepare for advanced airway management
  • Consider vasopressor support

Special Considerations

  • Biphasic Reaction: Monitor for minimum 4-6 hours; reactions may recur
  • Patients on Beta-Blockers: May require higher doses of epinephrine; consider glucagon
  • Pregnant Patients: Left lateral tilt position; standard medications still indicated
  • Difficult IV Access: IO access or continued IM epinephrine acceptable

Documentation Requirements

  • Time of symptom onset
  • Known or suspected allergen
  • All medications administered with times and doses
  • Vital signs before and after each intervention
  • Patient response to treatment
  • Medical direction contact (if applicable)

Transport Considerations

  • All anaphylaxis patients require hospital evaluation
  • Transport priority based on severity and response to treatment
  • Continue monitoring and be prepared for biphasic reaction
  • Bring all medications and suspected allergen samples (if safe)

Patient Education

  • Importance of carrying epinephrine auto-injector
  • Allergen avoidance strategies
  • Medical alert identification
  • Follow-up with allergist/immunologist
