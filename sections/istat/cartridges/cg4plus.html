CG4+

Blood gas and lactate cartridge for respiratory failure, shock, and sepsis decisions.

Purpose

  • pH, pCO₂, pO₂, bicarbonate, base excess, and lactate from a small whole-blood sample.
  • Guides ventilation/oxygenation, perfusion status, and resuscitation endpoints.

Supplies

  • CG4+ cartridge warmed to room temp.
  • Heparinized arterial or venous syringe (no air) or heparinized capillary tube for small draws.
  • Alcohol swab, gauze, PPE, and labels.

Storage & Handling

  • Store at 2–8°C; track expiry and out-of-fridge dates (room-temp limit commonly 14 days; verify lot insert).
  • Warm sealed pouch 5–10 minutes; avoid moisture/condensation.
  • Keep the cartridge level and open only when ready to fill.

QC Workflow

  1. Run required liquid controls for each new lot/shipment after bringing to room temp.
  2. Electronic simulator after transport or weekly if idle.
  3. Document lot numbers, PASS/FAIL, and operator; escalate repeated failures.

Patient Sample Workflow

  1. Draw arterial or venous blood into a heparinized syringe; expel all air bubbles and cap immediately.
  2. Gently roll/invert to mix; analyze as soon as possible (preferably <5 minutes for blood gas accuracy).
  3. Fill cartridge to the line smoothly without bubbles, close the cap, and insert within 30 seconds.
  4. Select patient ID/test type; hold analyzer level until results print.
  5. Interpret in context (e.g., lactate trend, ventilation changes) and document in the casualty record.

Common Errors & Fixes

  • Air bubbles (high pO₂/low CO₂): Flick out bubbles before filling; redraw if significant air remains.
  • Delayed analysis (lactate drift): Run immediately after draw; avoid letting the sample sit.
  • Underfill: Use a new cartridge and ensure steady fill; keep cartridge level.

Notes for Shipboard Use

  • Ideal for monitoring ventilated casualties and septic patients when lab is unavailable.
  • Collect and run at the bedside to minimize motion and time delay in sea states.
  • Print results and tape to the casualty chart; trend lactate and pH over time.
