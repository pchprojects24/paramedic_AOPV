Adverse Reactions & Anaphylaxis

Every immunization session must have anaphylaxis supplies immediately accessible. Know the signs and act without delay.

Types of Adverse Events Following Immunization (AEFI)

Common / Expected Reactions

  • Local: pain, redness, swelling at injection site — onset within hours, resolves in 1–3 days.
  • Systemic: low-grade fever, malaise, myalgia, headache — onset 6–24 hours, resolves in 1–2 days.
  • Management: cold compress to site, acetaminophen or ibuprofen for comfort. No treatment usually required.

Vasovagal Syncope (Fainting)

  • Signs: pallor, diaphoresis, lightheadedness, nausea, brief loss of consciousness.
  • Onset: usually within 15 minutes of injection (hence the observation period).
  • Management: lay the patient supine with legs elevated, protect from falling injury, monitor until fully recovered. This is not anaphylaxis.

Anaphylaxis

  • A severe, life-threatening systemic allergic reaction. Onset usually within minutes (up to 30 minutes) of vaccination.
  • Key signs: urticaria (hives) or angioedema, respiratory distress (stridor, wheeze, dyspnea), hypotension, tachycardia, GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, cramping), sense of impending doom.
  • Distinguishing from vasovagal: anaphylaxis involves urticaria/angioedema and/or respiratory compromise and/or hypotension; vasovagal typically involves bradycardia (not tachycardia) and no rash or respiratory distress.

Anaphylaxis Management Protocol

  1. Recognize it early — do not delay treatment waiting for "classic" presentation. If in doubt, treat as anaphylaxis.
  2. Call for help: Alert the primary immunizer and activate the emergency response as per unit SOP.
  3. Epinephrine IM — FIRST-LINE:
    • Adult dose: 0.5 mg (0.5 mL of 1:1000 concentration) IM into the mid-anterolateral thigh.
    • Auto-injector (EpiPen): 0.3 mg for adults — use through clothing if necessary.
    • Repeat every 5–15 minutes if symptoms persist or recur. There is no maximum number of doses in a life-threatening situation.
  4. Position: Lay supine with legs elevated unless respiratory distress requires a sitting position. Do not stand the patient up.
  5. Oxygen: High-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask if available.
  6. IV access: Establish if trained and equipped; administer normal saline bolus (1–2 L) for hypotension.
  7. Monitor: Continuous vital signs — HR, BP, SpO2, respiratory status.
  8. Secondary medications (adjuncts, not substitutes for epinephrine):
    • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 50 mg IM/IV — for urticaria/itching.
    • Salbutamol (Ventolin) nebulized — for bronchospasm not responding to epinephrine.
    • Consider corticosteroids (methylprednisolone) — may help prevent biphasic reaction but does not treat acute anaphylaxis.
  9. Observe: Minimum 4–6 hours post-reaction due to risk of biphasic anaphylaxis (recurrence of symptoms hours later).
  10. Arrange evacuation if on ship and the patient is not improving or if the reaction was severe.

Anaphylaxis Kit Contents

The following must be immediately available at every immunization session:

  • Epinephrine 1:1000 ampoules (at least 3) and/or EpiPen auto-injectors (at least 2).
  • Syringes (1 mL and 3 mL) and needles (25G x 25mm for IM).
  • Diphenhydramine 50 mg injectable.
  • Salbutamol MDI with spacer or nebulizer solution + nebulizer.
  • Oxygen source and non-rebreather mask.
  • Oral/nasal airway adjuncts.
  • Blood pressure cuff and stethoscope (or automated BP monitor).
  • Pulse oximeter.

AEFI Reporting

  • All serious adverse events (anaphylaxis, hospitalization, persistent disability, or any event of concern) must be reported.
  • Complete the CF AEFI reporting form and submit to CF H Svcs Gp within 24 hours for serious events.
  • Enter the event in CFHIS with full details: time of onset, symptoms, treatment given, and outcome.
  • Notify the primary immunizer immediately for any reaction beyond expected local/systemic effects.
