Cold Chain & Vaccine Storage

Maintaining the cold chain is critical to vaccine efficacy. Shipboard environments present unique challenges — vibration, power interruptions, and limited resupply.

Temperature Requirements

  • Standard vaccines: Store at +2 °C to +8 °C. This includes most inactivated, subunit, and toxoid vaccines.
  • Frozen vaccines (if carried): Some live vaccines (e.g., varicella, certain formulations) may require −15 °C or colder. Confirm product monograph.
  • Never freeze vaccines that are meant to be refrigerated — freezing destroys adjuvant-containing vaccines (Hep B, Tdap, HPV, etc.).
  • Diluents: Store at room temperature or refrigerated per product monograph. Never freeze diluents.

Daily Fridge Monitoring

  1. Check min/max thermometer twice daily (morning and end of day) and record readings on the fridge temperature log.
  2. If temperature has gone outside 2–8 °C range, do not administer affected vaccines. Quarantine them (mark "DO NOT USE") and contact the primary immunizer and pharmacy for a cold chain break assessment.
  3. Reset min/max thermometer after each reading.
  4. Ensure the fridge is a purpose-built pharmaceutical/vaccine fridge if available — not a bar fridge or domestic unit.
  5. Do not store food, drinks, or lab specimens in the vaccine fridge.

Cold Chain Break Procedures

  1. Immediately quarantine all affected vaccines — place in a separate, clearly marked container still within the fridge.
  2. Record the date, time, duration of the break, and observed temperature range.
  3. Contact CF H Svcs Gp pharmacy or the vaccine manufacturer for guidance on whether the product remains viable.
  4. Do not discard vaccines until directed — some products tolerate brief excursions.
  5. Document the event, the decision made, and the disposition of each affected lot.

Shipboard Considerations

  • Secure the fridge to prevent movement in heavy seas; ensure the door latch is reliable.
  • Connect the fridge to an uninterruptible power source or the ship's essential services circuit.
  • Carry a battery-operated data logger as a backup to the built-in thermometer.
  • When receiving vaccine resupply alongside, inspect packaging for cold-chain indicators (temperature strips, cold packs) and reject deliveries that show evidence of temperature excursion.
  • For transport between ship and shore clinic, use a validated insulated container with conditioned ice packs. Do not place vaccines directly against ice packs (risk of freezing).

Stock Management

  • Rotate stock using first-expiry-first-out (FEFO).
  • Check expiry dates monthly and remove expired products.
  • Keep an inventory log showing vaccine name, lot number, quantity, expiry date, and date received.
  • Coordinate resupply with the shore-side clinic or pharmacy well before stock runs low — account for sailing schedules and port availability.
