ZOLL Propaq MD

Quick-start guide and step-by-step instructions for first-time users and refreshers. This guide covers essential operations including monitoring, defibrillation, cardioversion, and pacing. Follow local protocols.

Essential startup procedure (2-3 minutes)

  1. Power and readiness: Connect to AC or confirm two charged batteries are installed. Press the On/Standby button and allow the self-test to complete. Verify date and time are correct.
  2. Patient setup: From the Home screen or Setup menu, select the correct patient category (Adult, Pediatric, or Neonate) so alarms scale appropriately. Attach ECG leads or multifunction pads, choose the appropriate lead (usually Lead II), and confirm a stable ECG tracing appears.
  3. Monitoring basics: Attach the SpO₂ sensor to the patient's finger and apply the NIBP cuff to the appropriate limb. Press the NIBP softkey for a manual blood pressure reading. In the NIBP menu, select an Auto interval (e.g., 15 minutes) or press STAT for an immediate cycle. Connect and start EtCO₂ monitoring if available and indicated.
  4. Set alarms: Press the Alarms key, highlight the desired parameter (ECG, SpO₂, NIBP), and use the selector knob to adjust high and low limits. Set alarm volume to an audible level. Note that Alarm Silence only pauses alarms temporarily.

You're ready to monitor! The device will now continuously display vital signs and alert you to changes outside the set parameters.

How to Set and Adjust Alarms

  1. Press the Alarms key on the device.
  2. Use the selector knob to highlight the parameter you want to adjust (ECG rate, SpO₂, systolic/diastolic BP, etc.).
  3. Turn the knob to set the high and low alarm limits to appropriate values for your patient.
  4. Adjust alarm volume under Alarm Volume or in the Setup menu to ensure it's audible.
  5. Remember: Alarm Silence only temporarily pauses alarms—they will reactivate after a set period.

How to Change NIBP Settings

  1. Press the NIBP softkey on the main screen.
  2. Select Manual for a one-time reading, Auto to set an automatic interval (e.g., every 5, 10, or 15 minutes), or STAT for an immediate repeated cycle.
  3. Ensure the cuff is properly sized and positioned on the patient's limb.
  4. Allow the cuff to fully deflate before attempting another reading to prevent false readings.

How to Adjust Display and Brightness

  1. Press the Display key to cycle through different screen layouts and waveform configurations.
  2. Use the brightness controls (usually a softkey or in Setup menu) to increase or decrease screen brightness for better visibility in different lighting conditions.
  3. Press the Size key to adjust waveform size for easier viewing of ECG traces.

How to Change Patient Settings

  1. Press Home or navigate to the Setup menu.
  2. Select Patient to access patient profile settings.
  3. Choose the appropriate patient category: Adult, Pediatric, or Neonate. This adjusts default alarm limits and settings.
  4. Adjust weight units (kg/lbs) as needed for your facility's protocols.
  5. Confirm that date, time, and default NIBP interval settings are correct.

How to Print Records

  1. Press the Record key to start or stop printing a continuous ECG strip.
  2. For 12-lead ECGs, the printout will automatically begin once the acquisition is complete.
  3. Ensure thermal paper is properly loaded and not expired for best print quality.
  4. Use printed strips for documentation, handoffs, or transmission to receiving facilities.

How to Manage Power and Batteries

  1. Always keep two charged batteries installed in the device for redundancy.
  2. Connect to AC/DC power whenever possible to preserve battery life.
  3. Monitor the battery indicator on screen; swap batteries before they fully deplete.
  4. Charge spare batteries using the approved ZOLL battery charger.
  5. Test battery status regularly during equipment checks.

Step-by-step defibrillation procedure:

  1. Prepare the patient: Ensure the patient is in cardiac arrest with a shockable rhythm (ventricular fibrillation or pulseless ventricular tachycardia). Apply multifunction defibrillation pads to the patient's bare chest—one on the upper right chest and one on the lower left lateral chest. Keep ECG monitoring active.
  2. Select defibrillation mode: Press the Defib key on the device. Ensure Sync mode is OFF (for defibrillation of VF/pulseless VT).
  3. Select energy level: Use the selector knob to choose the appropriate energy level (typically 120-200 Joules for biphasic, follow your protocols). The selected energy will display on screen.
  4. Charge the device: Press the Charge button (on the device or the pads). The device will emit an audible tone when fully charged.
  5. Clear the patient: Loudly announce "CLEAR" and perform a visual sweep to ensure no one is touching the patient, bed, or any conductive equipment.
  6. Deliver shock: Press the Shock button firmly (on the device or both buttons on the pads simultaneously). The device will deliver the shock.
  7. Post-shock: Immediately resume CPR starting with chest compressions. Reassess the rhythm after 2 minutes of CPR or per your resuscitation protocol. Repeat defibrillation if the shockable rhythm persists.

Important safety notes:

  • Never touch the patient during shock delivery.
  • Ensure the patient is on a non-conductive surface if possible.
  • Remove any medication patches from the chest and dry the skin before applying pads.
  • Follow your local cardiac arrest protocols for energy dosing and sequence.

Step-by-step cardioversion procedure:

  1. Patient preparation: Cardioversion is used for unstable tachycardias with a pulse (e.g., atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, SVT, or stable VT). If possible, sedate the patient per protocol. Apply multifunction pads to the patient's bare chest (anterolateral or anteroposterior position). Ensure continuous ECG monitoring is active with a clear tracing.
  2. Select cardioversion mode: Press the Defib key, then enable Sync mode. The device will display sync markers (usually small dots or arrows) on the ECG trace at each R wave.
  3. Confirm sync markers: Verify that the sync markers appear consistently on each QRS complex (R wave). If markers are not appearing correctly, adjust the ECG gain or lead selection until they are clearly visible.
  4. Select energy level: Use the selector knob to choose the appropriate starting energy based on the rhythm and your protocols (e.g., 50-100 J for atrial flutter, 120-200 J for atrial fibrillation).
  5. Charge and deliver: Press Charge. Once charged, announce "CLEAR" and ensure no one is touching the patient. Press the Shock button. The device will deliver the shock synchronized to the next R wave.
  6. Post-cardioversion: Immediately reassess the patient's rhythm and vital signs. If the arrhythmia persists, consider increasing energy and delivering another synchronized shock per protocol. Note that Sync mode may automatically turn OFF after shock delivery—re-enable it for subsequent shocks.

Key differences from defibrillation:

  • Sync mode must be ON for cardioversion to avoid delivering a shock on the T wave (which could induce VF).
  • Cardioversion is used for patients with a pulse; defibrillation is for pulseless arrest.
  • Lower energy levels are typically used initially for cardioversion compared to defibrillation.
  • The device will wait to deliver the shock on the R wave, which may cause a brief delay after pressing the shock button.

Step-by-step pacing procedure:

  1. Patient preparation: Transcutaneous pacing is used for symptomatic bradycardia unresponsive to medications. Apply multifunction pads to the patient's bare chest (anterolateral or anteroposterior position). If the patient is conscious, explain the procedure and consider analgesia/sedation per protocol, as pacing can be uncomfortable.
  2. Enter pacing mode: Press the Pacer key on the device. The pacing screen will appear.
  3. Select pacing mode: Choose between Demand mode (pacing occurs only when the patient's intrinsic rate falls below the set rate) or Fixed mode (pacing occurs at the set rate regardless of intrinsic rhythm). Demand mode is typically preferred.
  4. Set the pacing rate: Use the selector knob to set the desired heart rate (typically 60-80 bpm for adults, adjust based on patient needs and protocols).
  5. Set the output current (mA): Start with a low current (e.g., 30-40 mA) and prepare to increase. This determines the strength of the electrical impulse.
  6. Start pacing: Press Start Pacing. The device will begin delivering pacing impulses, visible as vertical lines (pacer spikes) on the ECG trace.
  7. Increase current to achieve capture: Gradually increase the current (mA) while watching the ECG for electrical and mechanical capture:
    • Electrical capture: Each pacer spike should be followed by a wide QRS complex.
    • Mechanical capture: Confirm by palpating a pulse synchronous with the paced rhythm or observing the patient's blood pressure.
  8. Set final current: Once capture is achieved, increase the current by an additional 10 mA above the capture threshold to ensure consistent capture. This is the operating current.
  9. Monitor the patient: Continuously monitor ECG, vital signs (especially blood pressure), and patient comfort. Adjust sedation as needed. Watch for loss of capture.
  10. Stop pacing: To discontinue pacing, press Stop Pacing. Monitor the patient's intrinsic rhythm closely after stopping.

Troubleshooting pacing issues:

  • No capture: Increase the current (mA). Check pad placement and contact with the skin. Consider changing pad position (try anteroposterior).
  • Patient discomfort: Pacing causes muscle contractions that can be painful. Provide analgesia/sedation per protocol. Reassure the patient.
  • Intermittent capture: Ensure pads are making good contact. Increase current slightly. Check for patient movement or poor pad adhesion.

No ECG Trace or Poor Signal

  • Verify all ECG lead cables are properly connected to the device and securely attached to the patient's electrodes.
  • Check electrode contact with the skin—replace electrodes if dry, expired, or not adhering well.
  • Try changing the selected lead (e.g., switch from Lead II to Lead III) to find a clearer signal.
  • Increase waveform size using the Size key and adjust brightness for better visibility.
  • Ensure the patient's skin is clean, dry, and free of excessive hair (clip or shave if necessary).
  • Check for electrical interference from other devices—move away from sources of artifact.

NIBP Reading Failed or Inaccurate

  • Verify the cuff is the correct size for the patient (bladder should cover 80% of arm circumference).
  • Check cuff placement—center the bladder over the brachial artery with the marking at proper position.
  • Ensure the limb is at heart level and the patient is still during the reading (movement causes errors).
  • Allow the cuff to fully deflate before attempting another reading.
  • Check for kinked tubing or disconnected hoses between the cuff and the device.
  • Try the other arm if readings continue to fail.
  • For very low or high blood pressures, the device may struggle—consider manual BP if needed.

SpO₂ Reading Unavailable or Unreliable

  • Ensure the SpO₂ sensor is properly positioned on the finger, toe, or earlobe with good contact.
  • Check for patient movement, shivering, or poor perfusion (cold extremities)—try a different site.
  • Verify the sensor is clean and dry; remove nail polish if using a finger sensor.
  • Replace the sensor if it appears damaged, contaminated, or is giving consistently poor readings.
  • Check the sensor cable connection to the device.
  • Consider alternate monitoring sites (earlobe, bridge of nose) in cases of poor peripheral perfusion.

EtCO₂ Reading Issues

  • Check that the sampling line is properly connected to the device and the airway adapter.
  • Verify there are no kinks, obstructions, or moisture in the sampling line.
  • Replace the sampling line or airway adapter if contaminated with secretions or water.
  • Ensure the patient has adequate ventilation—very low respiratory rates may affect readings.
  • Check that the CO₂ sensor is not expired or contaminated.

Battery Low or Power Issues

  • Swap in a charged battery pack immediately—always keep two batteries installed for redundancy.
  • Connect the device to AC/DC power whenever available to preserve battery life.
  • During equipment checks, test battery status and charge spare batteries using the approved charger.
  • If the device powers off unexpectedly, check battery contacts for corrosion or damage.
  • Keep batteries at room temperature; extreme cold or heat reduces battery performance.

Device Not Charging or Delivering Shock

  • Verify multifunction pads are properly applied to the patient's skin with good adhesion.
  • Check pad connections to the device—ensure they are fully seated in the port.
  • Confirm pads are not expired (check the expiration date on the package).
  • If the device fails to charge, check battery status and AC/DC power connection.
  • Ensure you are in the correct mode (Defib vs. Sync) for the intended therapy.
  • If the device displays an error message, consult the operator's manual or contact technical support.

Alarm Fatigue or Nuisance Alarms

  • Review alarm limits to ensure they are appropriately set for the patient's condition—avoid overly narrow limits.
  • Adjust alarm sensitivity if the device offers this option.
  • Ensure good signal quality (ECG, SpO₂) to reduce false alarms from artifact.
  • Never disable alarms entirely—adjust limits instead to reduce nuisance alarms while maintaining safety.
